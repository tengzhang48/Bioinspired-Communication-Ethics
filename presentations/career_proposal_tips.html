Research Plan Essentials

Demonstrating Technical Merit

‚úì DO:

  • Be ambitious yet believable
  • Show preliminary results
  • Include realistic timeline
  • State clear success metrics
  • Propose fundamental research

‚úó DON'T:

  • Use "develop," "design," "optimize"
  • Propose too much or too little
  • Be incremental
  • Leave methodology vague
  • Ignore seminal literature

Research Objective Formats (only 4 ways!):

  1. Test the hypothesis H
  2. Measure parameter P with accuracy A
  3. Prove the conjecture C
  4. Apply method M from field D to problem P in field E

Balance: ~2/3 research, ~1/3 education

Education Plan

More Than Just Teaching

‚ö†Ô∏è NOT Sufficient (too standard):

  • Developing a new graduate course
  • Working with graduate students
  • Including research in courses
  • Generic diversity statements

‚úì Strong Education Plans:

  • Integrate research and education seamlessly
  • Be unique and creative (TV programs, interactive tools, novel outreach)
  • Show assessment plans - how will you measure impact?
  • Use existing infrastructure - partner with campus centers, local schools
  • Cite education literature (10+ references)
  • Be specific - name contacts, describe actual activities

Focus on depth, not breadth

Do 2-3 things excellently rather than everything superficially

Intellectual Merit & Broader Impacts

NSF's Two Core Review Criteria

Intellectual Merit

  • What is the technical contribution to your field?
  • How does it advance knowledge across disciplines?
  • Connection to Grand Challenges or National Academies reports?
  • Why is this scientifically important NOW?

Broader Impacts

  • Benefits to society beyond your research field
  • Educational outreach (K-12, underrepresented groups)
  • Technology transfer and industry collaboration
  • Infrastructure development (tools, databases, etc.)

Make These EXPLICIT

Use headers, bold text, or dedicated sections. Don't make reviewers hunt for them!

Letters & Collaborations

Building Credibility Through Partners

Letters of Collaboration (NOT Support)

Strong letters demonstrate:

  • Knowledge of your work - they understand your research
  • Substantive commitment - money, equipment, data, personnel time
  • Clear relationship - specific plans for collaboration

Good Partners:

  • Industry collaborators
  • National labs
  • Other universities
  • Campus teaching centers
  • K-12 schools/museums

Value Added:

  • Validation of relevance
  • Access to data/facilities
  • Technology transfer
  • Cost-sharing support
  • Broader impact reach

Start building relationships 1+ year early

Strong partnerships take time to develop

Writing & Formatting

Professional Presentation Matters

Critical Formatting Rules:

  • Use 12-point font (Times Roman) - nothing smaller!
  • 1-inch margins (don't squeeze)
  • 15-page limit for Project Description
  • 1-page Project Summary
  • Follow NSF Grant Proposal Guide exactly

‚úì Good Practices:

  • High-quality figures
  • Bold key concepts
  • Clear section headers
  • No grammatical errors
  • Minimal technical jargon

‚úó Avoid:

  • Small, illegible fonts
  • Dense text walls
  • Excessive bullet points
  • Typos in title(!)
  • Poor quality images

Have multiple people proofread

Including technical experts AND non-technical readers

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Learning from Others' Experiences

1. Not following submission guidelines

NSF will return without review - read the GPG!

2. Treating it like a regular research proposal

This is about YOUR CAREER, not just a 5-year project

3. Marginalizing education component

Integration of research and education is the heart of CAREER

4. Being too incremental or too ambitious

Find the balance between impact and feasibility

5. Weak or generic letters of support

Get substantive commitments from collaborators

6. Submitting at the last minute

Technical problems or missing materials = return without review

The Review Process

Understanding Your Audience

Review Panel Composition

  • 3 assigned reviewers + full panel of ~15 experts
  • Mix of expertise levels in your specific area
  • Reading 10-20 proposals each
  • Limited time per proposal (~45 minutes)

What Reviewers Look For:

  • Clear research and career objectives (page 1!)
  • Feasibility and PI qualifications
  • Novel, fundamental contributions
  • Strong integration of research/education
  • Realistic timeline and methodology
  • Evidence of preliminary work

Reviewers will ask:

"Why should we invest in THIS person to do THIS work?"

Success = Making reviewers excited

"Gee, I wish I thought of that!"

If Your Proposal is Rejected

Strategies for Resubmission

Most PIs Need 2-3 Attempts

Success rate is ~10-15%, so rejection is common even for excellent proposals

After Rejection:

  • Read ALL reviewer comments carefully
  • Meet with program director to discuss feedback
  • Consider rewriting from scratch (not just revising)
  • Get more preliminary data/results
  • Strengthen letters and collaborations
  • Have 5+ people review next version

Common Reasons for Rejection:

  • Incremental rather than transformative
  • Unclear objectives or methodology
  • Scope too broad or too narrow
  • Weak education plan
  • Missing key literature
  • Lack of preliminary results
