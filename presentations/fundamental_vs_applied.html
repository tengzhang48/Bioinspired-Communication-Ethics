Why Language Shapes How Reviewers Perceive Your Research

NSF's Fundamental Question:

"Is this proposal advancing fundamental knowledge (research) or creating specific solutions (development)?"

‚ö†Ô∏è The Problem:

Certain words automatically signal "development" to reviewers, even if your work IS fundamental research. Your job is to frame it correctly.

Words That Signal "Not Research"

According to NSF Program Directors

Why These Words Are Problematic:

Word What Reviewers Think
"Develop" Creating a product/tool (engineering development)
"Design" Creating a specific solution (applied work)
"Optimize" Incremental improvement, not new knowledge
"Build/Create" Construction, not discovery
"Improve" Incremental, not transformative

NSF's Mandate:

"NSF specializes in funding fundamental research. NSF does NOT support development."

Applied vs. Fundamental Research

Understanding the Core Distinction

‚ùå Applied/Development
  • Creates specific solutions
  • Solves particular problems
  • Produces products or tools
  • Demonstrates feasibility
  • Context-specific
  • Outcome: "We have a thing"
"Design a more efficient turbine blade for wind energy"
‚úì Fundamental Research
  • Discovers principles
  • Explains mechanisms
  • Tests hypotheses
  • Establishes bounds/limits
  • Generalizable knowledge
  • Outcome: "We understand why"
"Test the hypothesis that vortex generators reduce drag by 25% through boundary layer energization"

The Key Distinction:

Applied: "I want to design a better X"

Fundamental: "I want to understand the principles that enable effective design of X"

The Framework That Works

Distinguishing the Activity from the Knowledge

üéØ Critical Insight:

Design itself ‚Üí Applied

Discovering design principles ‚Üí Fundamental

Optimizing performance ‚Üí Applied

Understanding why optimal solutions exist ‚Üí Fundamental

Ask Yourself These Questions:

If this works, what will we LEARN? (not just "what will we have?")
What fundamental question am I answering?
Will the knowledge transfer to other contexts?
Am I discovering principles or implementing solutions?

The Litmus Test:

Can you frame your objective using one of these four formats?

  1. Test the hypothesis H
  2. Measure parameter P with accuracy A
  3. Prove the conjecture C
  4. Apply method M from field D to problem P in field E

If yes ‚Üí You're framing it as fundamental research

Navigating the Gray Area

When Design/Optimization IS Research

Important Nuance:

You CAN do research on design, optimization, and control‚Äî
just frame it in terms of fundamental questions, not products.

From the NSF Guidelines:

"Understanding and applying the mathematics of [engineering decisions] is also important. This includes the application of probability theory, decision theory, game theory, optimization, control theory... This also is a legitimate area of research for engineering."

‚úì Acceptable When You're Studying:

  • The principles underlying design
  • The mechanisms that explain optimization
  • The theoretical bounds of what's achievable
  • The fundamental tradeoffs in complex systems
  • The mathematical properties of control strategies
  • The conditions under which approaches succeed/fail

‚ö†Ô∏è The Test:

Question: "If I complete this work successfully, what will the scientific community have learned that they didn't know before?"

Good answer: New principles, bounds, mechanisms, or understanding
Bad answer: A working prototype or improved performance metric

Key Takeaways

Remember These Points

Words trigger mental categories in reviewers' minds.
You have ~45 minutes to make your case.
Choose your words strategically.

1. The Distinction That Matters:

Design/optimize/develop something = Applied
Discover principles/mechanisms/bounds = Fundamental

2. The Reframing Strategy:

Don't describe the activity (designing, building)
Describe the knowledge you'll create (understanding, discovering)

3. The Question to Always Ask:

"If this works, what will we LEARN that we didn't know before?"

4. Your Mission as Educators:

Teach students to think and write like fundamental researchers, not just practitioners. The language shapes how they conceptualize their work.
