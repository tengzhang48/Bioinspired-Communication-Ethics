# Module 2: Scientific Writing & Peer Review  

This module provides comprehensive training in scientific communication through writing and the peer review process. Across seven lectures, you'll learn evidence-based strategies for effective scientific writing, practice transparent peer review techniques, examine how AI tools interact with scientific writing, and develop skills to respond constructively to reviewer feedback—all grounded in real case studies and your own survey responses.

## Lecture 1: Your Writing Journey
{: #lecture-1-your-writing-journey}

> **Objective:** Establish a shared baseline of the cohort's writing experience, identify common challenges, and frame scientific writing as a learnable, structured skill—not a mysterious talent.

### 📊 What Your Survey Told Us

Your Mentimeter responses reveal an important starting point:

- **Publication experience:** The majority of you (8/15) have zero publications so far, while 5 have 1–2. That's completely normal for this stage—this module is designed to accelerate your path from here.
- **"Enough" for a PhD:** Most of you (13/16) said 3–5 papers. That's a reasonable target, and the writing principles in this module will help you get there more efficiently.
- **Biggest pain points:** The word cloud highlighted **"idea," "introduction," "deadlines," and "revisions"** as the hardest parts of writing. We'll address each of these directly.
- **Support you want most:** **Feedback, collaboration, and good mentoring** dominated your responses. This module builds in peer feedback at every stage.
- **Reading habits:** Most of you (14/21) read papers weekly, and you read the whole paper but quickly (9/21). Only 1 person annotates deeply. We'll work on strategic reading skills.

### 📖 Pre-Class Reading

- 📄 [**"Whitesides' Group: How to Write a Paper"**](https://www.gmwgroup.harvard.edu/publications/whitesides-group-writing-paper)  
  The foundational guide that frames a paper as a *communication tool*, not just a documentation exercise.

### 🎯 In-Class Activities

- **Mentimeter warm-up:** Share your publication experience and biggest writing frustration (builds the data above).
- **Discussion:** Why is "the introduction" so painful? Connect to the gap between *knowing your work* and *communicating it to others*.
- **CARS exercise:** Read a poorly structured introduction. In teams, identify what's missing using the CARS model (Create A Research Space).
- **Speed-reading challenge:** Your survey showed 9/21 read the whole paper quickly, 7/21 read deeply, but only 1 annotates. To build strategic reading skills, each team reads the *same* paper at a different depth level: Team 1 reads title & abstract only (2 min), Team 2 reads intro & conclusion (5 min), Team 3 skims full paper (10 min), Team 4 reads deeply (15 min), Team 5 annotates key sections (15 min). Teams then answer the same 5 questions about the paper. Compare: How much did reading depth actually matter for each question? When is skimming enough, and when do you need deep reading?

### 📋 Writing Portfolio: Getting Started

> Throughout this module, each team will develop a **Writing Portfolio** — a single research abstract that evolves across lectures. By Lecture 7, you'll have a polished abstract that demonstrates every principle we cover.

**Lecture 1 task:** Each team selects a research topic from their members' work and writes a rough 1-paragraph description of the research question. This is your starting point — no formatting rules yet.

## Lecture 2: Writing Principles in Action
{: #lecture-2-writing-principles-in-action}

> **Objective:** Master the core writing workflow—conclusion first, outline-driven, figure-led—and confront which principles feel counterintuitive.

### ▶️ Interactive Presentation

- 📊 [**Writing Scientific Papers: Key Principles from Whitesides, Weitz & Suo Labs**](presentations/writing-principles.html)

### 📊 What Your Survey Told Us

Your agreement ratings on writing principles revealed interesting tensions:

| Principle | Agreement (1–5) | Your Take |
|-----------|:---:|-----------|
| Figures should be chosen before writing text | **4.4** | Strong consensus—visual storytelling resonates |
| Abstract should be written last | **4.1** | Most agree, but some resist |
| Start writing when you have a hypothesis | **3.4** | Split opinions—connects to hypothesis debate in Lecture 7 |
| Never use parentheses in main text | **2.7** | Controversial! Field-dependent |
| Write conclusion first | **2.6** | Most disagreed—yet this is the experts' top recommendation |

The hardest principle to implement? **"Write conclusion first"** (11/19), followed by **"Start writing immediately"** (6/19). Both challenge the instinct to wait for "complete" data.

And yet — when asked "In your field, when do people typically start writing papers?", 10/16 said **"it varies widely"**, only 4 said "as soon as initial results suggest a story," and 2 said "after all experiments are finished." Nobody said "after presenting at a conference." The Whitesides principle — *start writing immediately* — contradicts what most of you observe in practice. That tension is exactly what makes this lecture productive.

### 📖 Pre-Class Reading

- 🎥 [**"How to Write a Paper" – Prof. Whitesides Video Tutorial**](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=q3mrRH2aS98)
- 📄 [**TechRabbits: Complete Guide to Scientific Publication**](https://www.hyunwooyuk.com/table-of-contents.html)

### 🎯 In-Class Activities

- **Mentimeter:** Rate your agreement with the 5 controversial principles (generates the data above). Reveal and discuss where disagreement is highest.
- **Mini-debate:** "Should you write your conclusion first, or does that bias your analysis?" Teams argue both sides.
- **"Reviewer Simulator":** Each team receives a badly written paragraph. Identify problems using the principles, then rewrite in 10 minutes. Compare versions.

### 📋 Writing Portfolio: Draft 1

**Lecture 2 task:** Using the principles from today, each team writes a first-draft abstract for their chosen research topic. Requirements: must include a conclusion statement (1–2 sentences) written *first*, and at least one figure concept described in words. Bring to Lecture 3 for revision practice.

## Lecture 3: The Art of Revision
{: #lecture-3-the-art-of-revision}

> **Objective:** See how a real paper's abstract evolved through 4 revisions—from initial submission to Nature publication—and understand *why* each change was made.

### 📋 Case Study: "Tough Bonding of Hydrogels to Diverse Nonporous Surfaces"

This abstract went through four major revisions before publication in *Nature Materials*. By tracking what changed at each stage, you'll see the writing principles from Lecture 2 in action.

**Key evolution across 4 versions:**

| Version | Title Change | Major Abstract Revisions |
|---------|-------------|------------------------|
| Initial | "Multifunctional bonding..." (descriptive) | Focused on strategy + method; mechanism described abstractly |
| 1st Revision | Same title | Added quantitative benchmark (∼800 J/m²); simplified mechanism description |
| 2nd Revision | Same title | Added "silanation" as specific method; expanded mechanistic explanation |
| 3rd Revision | **"Tough Bonding of Hydrogels..."** (concise, impactful) | Restructured entirely—cleaner narrative flow, stronger opening hook, removed "multifunctional" |

**What the revisions teach:**
- **Title:** Evolved from a description of features to a statement of the achievement
- **Opening hook:** Shifted from "whereas..." (defensive) to an active comparison with nature
- **Mechanism clarity:** Each revision added more specific detail (from vague "strategy" → "covalent anchoring" → "silanation")
- **Narrative arc:** Final version follows a clear story: nature's benchmark → our approach → how it works → what it achieves → applications

### 📖 Materials

- ▶️ [**The Art of Revision: An Abstract Case Study**](presentations/abstract-evolution.html) — Interactive side-by-side comparison of all 4 versions
- 📄 Abstract revision history (4 versions) — provided in class
- 📄 [**"The Science of Scientific Writing" – American Scientist**](https://www.americanscientist.org/article/the-science-of-scientific-writing)

### 🎯 In-Class Activities

- **Side-by-side analysis:** Teams compare Version 1 and Version 4. List every change and categorize: *clarity*, *specificity*, *narrative structure*, or *scope*.
- **The "2-2-1" test:** Does the final abstract follow Weitz Lab's formula (2 intro sentences, 2 result sentences, 1 significance sentence)? Where does it deviate and why?
- **Your turn:** Each team applies the same revision lens to their own Writing Portfolio abstract from Lecture 2. What needs to change?

### 📋 Writing Portfolio: Draft 2

**Lecture 3 task:** Revise your team abstract based on the revision principles from today's case study. Focus on: title (descriptive → impactful), opening hook, mechanism clarity, and narrative arc. Bring to Lecture 5 for peer review.

## Lecture 4: Peer Review & Transparent Publishing
{: #lecture-4-peer-review--transparent-publishing}

> **Objective:** Understand how peer review works, what transparent review changes, and form evidence-based opinions about modern publishing models.

### 📊 What Your Survey Told Us

Your familiarity with these concepts varied significantly:

- **eLife:** 9/17 had never heard of it; only 2 knew about the "Reviewed Preprint" model
- **Open Access:** 12/18 understand the basic idea, but none could explain different models (Gold, Green, etc.)
- **Transparent peer review:** Split three ways—3 never heard of it, 7 heard the term, 6 understand the idea, 0 have direct experience

**What you valued most about eLife's model:**
- Public reviews (9/17) — seeing the full conversation between reviewers and authors
- eLife Assessment replacing accept/reject (4/17)

**What concerned you most:**
- Eliminating accept/reject decisions (12/18) — the top concern by far
- Predatory journals exploiting the open access model (12/20)
- Quality potentially dropping (4/20)

**Open Access associations:** "researcher friendly," "free," "visibility," "equity," "shared information"

**Top Open Access benefit:** Faster access to research for everyone (9/17)
**Top Open Access concern:** Rise of predatory journals (12/20)

### 📖 Pre-Class Reading

- 📄 [**eLife: Transparent Review Process**](https://elifesciences.org/inside-elife/3d8c981b/transparent-peer-review-at-elife)
- 📄 [**COPE: Ethical Guidelines for Peer Reviewers**](https://publicationethics.org/files/Ethical_Guidelines_For_Peer_Reviewers.pdf)

### 🎯 In-Class Activities

- **Mentimeter:** Gauge familiarity with eLife, Open Access, and transparent review (generates the data above).
- **Case study:** Read the transparent peer review of [**"Autonomous self-burying seed carriers for aerial seeding"**](https://elifesciences.org/articles/87398#public-review) (8/12 of you read it; 4 did not—team up for cross-teaching).
- **Team analysis:** Each team answers: (1) What was the most challenging reviewer comment? (2) How did authors respond? (3) Was transparency helpful or harmful here?
- **Closing poll:** "After seeing this example, are you more or less supportive of transparent peer review?"

> 🔗 **Connection to Module 4 (Ethics):** Transparent peer review raises fundamental ethical questions: Does reviewer anonymity protect honest critique, or enable abuse of power? What happens when early-career researchers review senior colleagues' work publicly? We'll return to these tensions in the Ethics module when we discuss systemic pressures in science (Alberts et al.).

## Lecture 5: Responding to Reviewers
{: #lecture-5-responding-to-reviewers}

> **Objective:** Develop professional strategies for responding to reviewer comments—including how to disagree respectfully with evidence.

### 📊 What Your Survey Told Us

From your analysis of the seed carrier paper's transparent review:

- **Most challenging comment type:** Requests for additional experiments (8/14), followed by statistical/reproducibility concerns (3/14)
- **Most effective response strategy:** Respectful, evidence-based rebuttals when disagreeing (11/12) — unanimous consensus
- **Authors' attitude toward reviewers:** "Respectful" and "professional" dominated the word cloud, with "specific" and "clarifying" also prominent
- **Most surprising aspect:** How much new work the authors had to do (8/15)

**Peer Review Reflection (scale ratings):**

| Question | Average |
|----------|:---:|
| How well did authors handle Referee #2's challenge about necessity? | **4.5** / 5 |
| How much did the manuscript improve through review? | **4.2** / 5 |
| How confident do you feel handling major reviewer comments? | **3.6** / 5 |

That 3.6 confidence score is exactly why we're spending a full lecture on this.

### 📖 Pre-Class Reading

- 📄 [**TechRabbits: The Art of Rebuttal**](https://www.hyunwooyuk.com/table-of-contents.html)
- 📄 [**How to Respond to Reviewer Comments – Nature Guide**](https://www.nature.com/scitable/blog/mental_elixir/how_to_respond_to_peer_reviewers/)

### 🎯 In-Class Activities

- **Anatomy of a rebuttal:** Dissect 3 real reviewer comments and author responses from the seed carrier paper. Categorize each response as: *agreed and changed*, *agreed partially*, or *respectfully disagreed with evidence*.
- **Response drafting exercise:** Each team receives a challenging reviewer comment (drawn from real reviews). Draft a response in 15 minutes. Teams then peer-review each other's responses.
- **The "tone test":** Compare two responses to the same comment—one defensive, one professional. Discuss what makes the difference.
- **Mentimeter closing:** "What's your confidence level for handling reviewer comments now?" (Compare with the 3.6 baseline)

### 📋 Writing Portfolio: Peer Review

**Lecture 5 task:** Each team submits their Draft 2 abstract. Another team acts as "reviewers" — writing 3 specific comments (1 major, 2 minor) using the constructive review principles from today. The authoring team then drafts a formal response to each comment. Both the review and the response are graded.

## Lecture 6: AI Tools in Scientific Writing
{: #lecture-6-ai-tools-in-scientific-writing}

> **Objective:** Critically evaluate what AI tools can and cannot do in scientific writing—using a concrete case study that exposes both capabilities and dangerous failures.

### 📋 Case Study: Two AI Models Evaluate the Same Abstract

Remember the hydrogel abstract we traced through 4 revisions in Lecture 3? We gave the final published version to ChatGPT (GPT-5) and DeepSeek (V3.1) and asked: *"Is this a good abstract?"* This creates a direct comparison — you already know what makes this abstract strong from human revision. Now see how AI evaluates it.

### ▶️ Interactive Presentation

- 📊 [**AI Tools in Scientific Writing: Capabilities and Failures**](presentations/ai-writing-comparison.html) — Side-by-side AI responses with error annotations

**What both AI models got right:**
- Identified the IMRaD structure correctly
- Recognized the bio-inspired framing as effective
- Praised the quantitative benchmarks
- Offered reasonable stylistic suggestions

**Where things went wrong—hallucinations and errors:**

| | ChatGPT (GPT-5) | DeepSeek (V3.1) |
|--|-----------------|-----------------|
| **Authors** | Correctly identified after follow-up | Listed wrong authors (Wang, Li, Suo) — confused with a different paper |
| **Lab** | Correctly identified Zhao group at MIT | Incorrectly attributed to Suo group at Harvard |
| **Year** | Correctly identified | Initially wrong, then self-corrected to *another wrong answer* |
| **Title** | Correct | First gave wrong title, then corrected to yet another wrong title |
| **Journal** | Correct | Correct (Nature) but wrong volume/page |

DeepSeek's response is particularly instructive: it *confidently* provided incorrect details, then when asked to fact-check itself, produced an apologetic correction that was *also wrong*—attributing the paper to a completely different research group.

**Key teaching points:**
- AI is good at structural analysis and stylistic feedback
- AI is unreliable for factual attribution (authors, dates, journals)
- Confident tone ≠ accurate content
- "Self-correction" can compound errors rather than fix them
- Connects to research ethics: what happens if you cite based on AI-generated references?

### 🎯 In-Class Activities

- **Reveal exercise:** Show the AI responses side by side. Teams identify what's correct vs. hallucinated *before* seeing the answer key.
- **Test it yourself:** Each team submits an abstract from their field to an AI tool. Evaluate the feedback: What's useful? What's wrong? What's missing?
- **Ethics discussion:** If AI can't reliably identify authors of a paper it's analyzing, what does that mean for using AI to write literature reviews? Where do you draw the line?
- **Mentimeter:** "After this exercise, how much do you trust AI feedback on scientific writing?" (Scale 1–5)

> 🔗 **Connection to Module 4 (Ethics):** AI hallucinations in citation are a research integrity issue. If a student uses AI-generated references without verification, is that fabrication? Negligence? We'll explore this further in the Ethics module alongside discussions of responsible AI use in research (Resnik's principles of honesty and due diligence).

### 📋 Writing Portfolio: AI Stress Test

**Lecture 6 task:** Each team submits their revised abstract to an AI tool and evaluates the feedback. What did AI get right? What did it miss that your human peer reviewers caught? Write a 1-paragraph reflection comparing AI vs. human feedback quality.

## Lecture 7: From Hypothesis to Publication
{: #lecture-7-from-hypothesis-to-publication}

> **Objective:** Connect writing skills to research design by examining how different research approaches shape what and how you write—bridging to Module 3 (Proposal Writing).

### 📊 What Your Survey Told Us

Your responses reveal a cohort that values hypothesis-driven research but recognizes its limits:

- **Research approach:** Primarily hypothesis-driven (9/18), followed by method/technology development (4/18) and mixed (3/18). Only 2 described themselves as primarily exploratory.
- **Hypothesis importance:** Rated 4.7/5 for quality research—near-unanimous agreement. But when asked if *all* research needs a hypothesis, Statements 2 and 3 scored only 1.7 and 1.8—strong disagreement.
- **What else drives research:** "Exploration," "observation," and "test" dominated the word cloud—exactly the approaches that don't fit neatly into hypothesis-first frameworks.
- **Nobel Prize estimate:** You guessed 73.8% of Nobel-winning research is hypothesis-driven. The actual answer (which varies by field) is a productive surprise.
- **Which field has more accidental discoveries?** Split between Chemistry (8) and Physiology/Medicine (8). The data shows Physiology/Medicine leads at ∼55% serendipitous.
- **Which examples truly need a hypothesis?** Only Discovery Science got a vote (1). Method Development, Exploratory Research, and Applied Problem-Solving all scored 0—you already recognize these don't require traditional hypotheses.

**Where do your research ideas come from?** This is perhaps the most revealing data point: 16/18 said their research idea came from **their academic advisor or PI**. Only 2 said "myself, based on my own curiosity." Zero came from brainstorming sessions, class projects, literature gaps, or existing projects they joined. That's completely normal at this stage — but it highlights a skill gap that Module 3 (Proposal Writing) directly addresses. Writing a proposal requires you to generate and defend *your own* research question, which is a fundamentally different task from executing your advisor's idea.

### ▶️ Interactive Presentations

- 📊 [**What is a Hypothesis?**](presentations/hypothesis-definitions.html)
- 📊 [**Nobel Prize Analysis: How Scientific Breakthroughs Really Happen**](presentations/nobel-analysis.html)

### 📖 Pre-Class Reading

- 📄 [**Nature: How to Review a Paper**](https://www.nature.com/nature-portfolio/editorial-policies/how-review-paper) — Read with an eye toward how hypothesis clarity affects reviewability

### 🎯 In-Class Activities

- **Mentimeter:** Estimate what % of Nobel-winning research is hypothesis-driven. Reveal and discuss the actual data by field.
- **Team exercise:** Each team categorizes 5 famous discoveries as hypothesis-driven, serendipitous, or method-driven. Compare with the research literature.
- **Writing connection:** "How does your research approach change how you write your introduction?" Teams draft two versions of the same introduction—one hypothesis-framed, one exploration-framed.
- **Bridge to Module 3:** Preview how these same principles apply to proposal writing. What does NSF look for in a hypothesis statement? How do you frame exploratory work in a proposal? The research idea origin data (16/18 from advisors) shows why this transition matters — proposals require you to generate your own questions.

It should reflect all revisions from Lectures 2–6: conclusion-first structure, clear narrative arc, peer review responses incorporated, and a brief note on how AI feedback did (or didn't) improve it. Include a 1-page revision history showing how the abstract evolved across drafts.

## 📚 Module Activities & Learning Objectives

Through seven lectures and guided practice, students will develop skills in:

- **Scientific Manuscript Writing:** Applying structured frameworks (conclusion-first, outline-driven, figure-led) to craft clear abstracts and introductions for bioinspired research.
- **Critical Analysis of Revision:** Tracking how real papers evolve through peer review, understanding *why* changes improve communication.
- **Constructive Peer Review:** Evaluating manuscripts using established criteria, providing specific, actionable, and respectful feedback.
- **Professional Response to Review:** Crafting evidence-based responses to reviewer comments, including respectful disagreement.
- **AI Literacy in Writing:** Evaluating AI writing tools critically, recognizing capabilities and limitations (especially hallucinations in attribution).
- **Research Design & Communication:** Connecting research approach (hypothesis-driven, exploratory, method-development) to writing strategy.

### 📝 Writing Portfolio (Team Assignment)

The central deliverable for this module is a **team Writing Portfolio** — a research abstract that evolves across all seven lectures:

| Lecture | Portfolio Milestone |
|:---:|---------------------|
| 1 | Select topic & write rough research description |
| 2 | Draft 1: Apply conclusion-first, outline-driven principles |
| 3 | Draft 2: Revise using abstract evolution case study lessons |
| 5 | Peer Review: Another team reviews your abstract; you respond formally |
| 6 | AI Stress Test: Submit to AI, compare feedback with human review |
| 7 | Final Version: Polished abstract + 1-page revision history |

This mirrors the real publication cycle: draft → revise → peer review → respond → revise again — giving each team hands-on experience with the complete scientific communication process.
